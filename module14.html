Lecture

Key concepts

Clergy should refer when: Ongoing abuse is disclosed In pastoral care, people often use spiritual language to describe pain that actually began in the home. The goal here is not to label families as “bad,” but to recognize repeating patterns that create fear, shame, confusion, or emotional isolation—so you can respond with wisdom and care.

Open the full lecture

When we say a family is dysfunctional, we mean the system repeatedly fails at the tasks a healthy family should do: create safety, allow emotions, manage conflict, set appropriate boundaries, and form identities that are not controlled by fear or role expectations.

This module’s slide outline highlights key signals to listen for: Trauma symptoms are severe Addiction is active Marriage involves coercion or control Mental illness is impairing functioning Referrals are not failure—they are wisdom..

A useful analytic framework for learners is: (1) identify the pattern, (2) ask what the pattern protects, (3) name the cost (spiritual, relational, emotional), and (4) choose the smallest safe step toward truth and connection. This keeps pastoral care grounded, compassionate, and practical.

Two lanes guide our work throughout the course. Clinical: nervous-system safety, trauma responses, boundaries, and referral when the need exceeds pastoral scope. Biblical: imago Dei, truth spoken in love, repentance and repair, and the distinction between forgiveness and ongoing access.

Skill Lab

Say it like this

Skill lab: helpful language
  • “I’m glad you told me. Your safety matters.”
  • “I can’t hold this alone—let’s bring in appropriate help.”
  • “Referral is wisdom, not rejection.”
  • “We can walk with you through the next steps.”

Avoid:

  • “I promised confidentiality, so I can’t involve anyone.”
  • “You’re overreacting.”
  • “Just read your Bible more.”
